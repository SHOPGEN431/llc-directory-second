LLC Formation Guide

Complete step-by-step guide to forming your Limited Liability Company

What is an LLC?

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) is a business structure that combines the liability protection of a corporation with the tax benefits and flexibility of a partnership. It's one of the most popular business structures for small businesses and entrepreneurs.

Step-by-Step LLC Formation Process

1 Choose Your State of Formation

Decide where to form your LLC. You can form in:

  • Your home state: If you'll be doing business primarily in one state
  • Delaware: For businesses that want maximum legal protection and flexibility
  • Wyoming: For privacy and tax benefits
Important: If you form in a different state than where you operate, you'll need to register as a "foreign LLC" in your home state.

2 Choose Your LLC Name

Your LLC name must be unique and comply with state requirements:

  • Must include "LLC," "L.L.C.," or "Limited Liability Company"
  • Cannot be the same as existing businesses in your state
  • Avoid restricted words (Bank, Insurance, etc.)
  • Check availability with your state's business registry
Good Examples:
  • Smith Consulting LLC
  • ABC Services, L.L.C.
  • Tech Solutions Limited Liability Company
Avoid:
  • Generic names like "Business LLC"
  • Names that are too similar to existing businesses
  • Names with restricted words

3 Appoint a Registered Agent

A registered agent receives legal documents on behalf of your LLC:

  • Must have a physical address in your formation state
  • Must be available during business hours
  • Can be yourself, a business partner, or a professional service
  • Many LLC formation services include registered agent service
Tip: Professional registered agent services offer privacy protection and ensure you never miss important legal documents.

4 File Articles of Organization

This is the official document that creates your LLC:

  • File with your state's business registration office
  • Include basic information about your LLC
  • Pay the required filing fee (varies by state)
  • Processing time varies (1-10 business days)
Required Information:
  • LLC name
  • Registered agent information
  • Business purpose (optional in some states)
  • Management structure
Filing Fees (Examples):
  • Delaware: $90
  • California: $70
  • Texas: $300
  • New York: $200

5 Create an Operating Agreement

An operating agreement outlines how your LLC will be managed:

  • Required in some states, recommended in all
  • Defines ownership percentages
  • Outlines management structure
  • Establishes voting rights and procedures
  • Protects limited liability status
Important: Even single-member LLCs should have an operating agreement to maintain liability protection.

6 Obtain an EIN (Employer Identification Number)

An EIN is like a Social Security number for your business:

  • Free from the IRS
  • Required for hiring employees
  • Needed for business bank accounts
  • Used for tax filing
  • Protects your personal Social Security number
Apply for EIN Online

7 Open a Business Bank Account

Separate your business and personal finances:

  • Required to maintain liability protection
  • Makes accounting and tax filing easier
  • Professional appearance for customers
  • Better financial organization
Tip: Many banks offer special business account packages for new LLCs.

8 Obtain Required Licenses and Permits

Depending on your business type and location:

  • Business license from your city/county
  • Professional licenses (if applicable)
  • Industry-specific permits
  • Sales tax permit (if selling products)
Check License Requirements

Additional Considerations

Ongoing Requirements
  • Annual reports (most states)
  • State tax filings
  • Federal tax returns
  • Maintain registered agent
  • Keep records updated
Liability Protection
  • Personal assets protected
  • Business debts separate
  • Professional credibility
  • Tax flexibility
  • Easier to raise capital

When to Seek Professional Help

Consider hiring a professional if you have:

  • Multiple owners or complex ownership structure
  • Unusual business activities
  • Need for specialized tax planning
  • Complex compliance requirements
  • Questions about liability protection
