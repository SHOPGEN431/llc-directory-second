LLC Terminology Guide

Understanding LLC terminology is essential for making informed business decisions. This glossary provides clear definitions of common terms you'll encounter when forming and operating an LLC.

A - C

Articles of Organization

The official document filed with the state to create an LLC. Contains basic information about the company including name, address, registered agent, and purpose.

Annual Report

A yearly filing required by most states to update company information and maintain good standing. Includes current address, members, and business activities.

Asset Protection

The legal separation between business and personal assets that protects personal property from business liabilities and creditors.

Business License

A permit required by local governments to operate a business within their jurisdiction. Requirements vary by location and business type.

Capital Contribution

Money, property, or services contributed by members to the LLC in exchange for ownership interest. Can be made at formation or later.

Corporate Veil

The legal protection that separates business and personal liabilities. Can be "pierced" if the LLC is not properly maintained or operated.

D - F

Dissolution

The legal process of ending an LLC's existence. Can be voluntary (by members) or involuntary (by state action for non-compliance).

Distribution

Money or property distributed to LLC members from company profits. Distributions are typically made according to ownership percentages.

EIN (Employer Identification Number)

A federal tax identification number issued by the IRS. Required for hiring employees, opening business bank accounts, and filing tax returns.

Foreign LLC

An LLC formed in one state but doing business in another state. Must register as a "foreign LLC" in the new state.

Franchise Tax

An annual tax charged by some states for the privilege of doing business in that state. Amount varies by state and business size.

G - I

Good Standing

The status of an LLC that has met all state requirements including annual reports, taxes, and other filings. Required for business operations.

Guaranteed Payments

Payments made to LLC members for services rendered, regardless of company profits. Treated as business expenses for tax purposes.

Income Allocation

The distribution of LLC profits and losses among members according to their ownership percentages or as specified in the operating agreement.

Indemnification

Legal protection for LLC members and managers against personal liability for actions taken on behalf of the company.

J - L

Joint and Several Liability

A legal concept where multiple parties can be held responsible for the full amount of a debt or obligation, not just their portion.

Limited Liability

The protection that limits personal liability of LLC members to their investment in the company. Personal assets are generally protected from business debts.

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

A business structure that combines the liability protection of a corporation with the tax benefits and flexibility of a partnership.

Liquidation

The process of converting LLC assets to cash to pay creditors and distribute remaining funds to members upon dissolution.

M - O

Manager

A person appointed to manage the LLC's business affairs. Can be a member or non-member. Management structure is defined in the operating agreement.

Member

An owner of an LLC. Members have ownership rights and may participate in management depending on the LLC structure.

Member-Managed LLC

An LLC where all members participate in management decisions. Most common structure for small LLCs.

Operating Agreement

A legal document that outlines how the LLC will be managed, including member rights, profit distribution, and decision-making procedures.

Organizer

The person or entity responsible for filing the Articles of Organization with the state. Can be a member, attorney, or formation service.

P - R

Pass-Through Taxation

The default tax treatment for LLCs where business income "passes through" to members' personal tax returns, avoiding double taxation.

Piercing the Corporate Veil

A legal doctrine where courts disregard the LLC's separate legal status and hold members personally liable for business debts.

Principal Place of Business

The primary location where the LLC conducts its business operations. Used for legal and tax purposes.

Registered Agent

A person or company designated to receive legal documents on behalf of the LLC. Must have a physical address in the formation state.

Reinstatement

The process of restoring an LLC to good standing after it has been administratively dissolved for non-compliance.

S - U

S-Corporation Election

A tax election that allows an LLC to be taxed as an S-Corporation, potentially reducing self-employment taxes on distributions.

Self-Employment Tax

Social Security and Medicare taxes paid by business owners on their share of business income. Applies to LLC members under default taxation.

Single-Member LLC

An LLC with only one owner. Treated as a disregarded entity for federal tax purposes unless S-Corp election is made.

Statement of Information

An annual filing required in some states (like California) to update company information and maintain good standing.

Tax Classification

How the IRS treats an LLC for tax purposes. Options include disregarded entity, partnership, S-Corporation, or C-Corporation.

Unanimous Consent

Agreement by all LLC members on a particular decision. Required for certain major decisions unless otherwise specified in the operating agreement.

V - Z

Voting Rights

The right of LLC members to participate in company decisions. Typically proportional to ownership percentage unless modified in the operating agreement.

Winding Up

The process of settling LLC affairs before dissolution, including paying creditors, liquidating assets, and distributing remaining funds to members.

Withdrawal

The process of a member leaving the LLC. Procedures and consequences are typically outlined in the operating agreement.
