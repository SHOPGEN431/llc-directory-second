LLC Compliance Guide

Complete guide to maintaining your LLC's compliance and good standing

LLC Compliance Overview

While LLCs have fewer compliance requirements than corporations, they still have ongoing obligations to maintain their good standing and liability protection. Understanding and meeting these requirements is crucial for protecting your business and personal assets.

Annual Reports and State Filings

Annual Reports

Most states require LLCs to file annual reports to maintain good standing:

What's Required:
  • Basic company information
  • Registered agent details
  • Principal office address
  • Member/manager information
  • Business activities summary
Common Deadlines:
  • Delaware: March 1
  • California: By anniversary date
  • Texas: May 15
  • New York: By anniversary date
  • Florida: May 1
Important: Missing annual reports can result in late fees, penalties, and potential dissolution of your LLC.

State-Specific Compliance Requirements

Franchise Taxes

Some states charge franchise taxes or similar fees:

States with Franchise Taxes:
  • Delaware: $300 minimum
  • Texas: $0.375 per $1,000 of capital
  • California: $800 minimum franchise tax
  • New York: $25 minimum
  • Illinois: $250 minimum
States with No Franchise Tax:
  • Wyoming
  • Nevada
  • South Dakota
  • Alaska
  • New Hampshire

Business Licenses and Permits

Depending on your business type and location:

  • General business license: Required in most cities/counties
  • Professional licenses: For regulated professions
  • Industry-specific permits: For certain business types
  • Sales tax permit: If selling products or services
  • Zoning permits: For physical locations
Tip: Check with your local city/county clerk and state licensing board for specific requirements.

Record Keeping Requirements

Required Records

Legal Documents:
  • Articles of Organization
  • Operating Agreement
  • EIN documentation
  • Business licenses and permits
  • Annual reports
  • Tax returns and filings
Financial Records:
  • Bank statements and records
  • Income and expense records
  • Invoices and receipts
  • Payroll records (if applicable)
  • Asset purchase records
  • Loan agreements
Best Practice: Keep records for at least 7 years for tax purposes and maintain organized digital and physical copies.

Common Compliance Mistakes to Avoid

Critical Mistakes
  • Missing annual report deadlines
  • Not maintaining separate bank accounts
  • Failing to pay franchise taxes
  • Not updating member information
  • Mixing personal and business expenses
Common Oversights
  • Forgetting to renew licenses
  • Not keeping proper records
  • Missing estimated tax payments
  • Not updating operating agreement
  • Ignoring state-specific requirements

Consequences of Non-Compliance

Risks of Non-Compliance

Financial Consequences:
  • Late fees and penalties
  • Interest charges
  • Reinstatement fees
  • Loss of tax benefits
  • Legal costs
Legal Consequences:
  • Loss of liability protection
  • Administrative dissolution
  • Difficulty obtaining financing
  • Problems with contracts
  • Personal liability exposure
Warning: Non-compliance can pierce the corporate veil, exposing your personal assets to business liabilities.
